In this series I’ve argued that doing high quality work, fast, is a superpower[^1], that focusing on your craft, not politics, is critical to developing that superpower[^2], and mastering the basics is core to becoming good and fast [^3]. 

This post is about focus, but a more interesting way to frame focus is asking this question: 

> What can you ignore in order to focus on becoming fast and your work?

The answer is that you can ignore almost everything. 



---

The science behind focus is sobering. 

One distraction has x impact on completing a task. 

But the environment in which the modern knowledge worker operates is hostile to focus. 

In many companies, it feels hard (or impossible) to operate without having Slack open all day, which creates a downward spiral towards expectations of near-synchronous communication, real or felt. 

We are also personally distracted and a primary [culprit] is social media, which is intentionally designed to draw us in and keep us scrolling. 

## Distractedness is a ceiling on speed and skill (AI makes it worse)

The consequences of distraction clearly have an impact on how quickly things get done, impacting the speed component of being fast and good. 

But the more pernicious problem is that it limits you from building skill as well. We all know that our best work happens when we gan give it our full attention for extended periods of time, and that’s the very process that sharpens the mental tools required to do that work. 

AI exacerbates this problem. First, the form factor follows the dopamine-inducing style of text messaging. 

More importantly, though, its effectiveness and speed almost make the consequences of working distracted disappear. You feel like you are getting more done, and that’s likely true quantitatively, but you’re not learning or getting better at your craft in the process.
